# Three things converging in one window: defending a $1B specialty biologic.

*A working point of view, produced from a messy brief in ~75 minutes.*

---

There's a familiar shape to the conversation that happens around a specialty biologic in the last 18 months before biosimilar entry. The brand team has been delivering for years. The franchise is profitable, prescriber-loyal, and quietly running. And then three vectors arrive in the same window and everything that worked for the last decade becomes the wrong answer at the same time.

This is a working POV on one of those windows.

## The setup

A $1B US specialty inflammation biologic. The kind of asset that has shaped a category — strong rheumatology, dermatology, and gastroenterology adoption, durable prescriber relationships, and a payer book that a brand team has spent years cultivating.

Three things are about to happen in the next 18 months.

**First**, biosimilar entry in 12 months. First approval already on the calendar. Step-edit pressure already showing up in commercial books — payer coverage memos circulating, P&T committees scheduling reviews. The biosimilar pipeline behind the first entrant is deep enough that this is not a one-time event; it is the start of a multi-year transition.

**Second**, an adjacent indication launches in 6 months. Same molecule, different therapeutic position. Material upside if it lands well, but only if the parent franchise stays intact through the transition — because field force time, KOL attention, and budget are zero-sum in any single fiscal year.

**Third**, payer pressure on existing access is already escalating. Step edits, prior authorization tightening, formulary tier movement on commercial books. None of this is yet visible in NRx — but the leading indicators are unambiguous.

Each of these is manageable on its own. Together, they reshape the franchise.

## Why prescriber loyalty alone won't hold this

The instinct in conversations like this is to lean on prescriber loyalty. *We have great relationships. The KOLs are with us. The reps are seasoned. The brand has equity.* All true. None of it sufficient.

The Remicade lesson is the one that should haunt every defense conversation. Biosimilars took **seven years** to reach 50% of Remicade share. And Remicade still went from $4.8B in US revenue in 2016 to $0.9B in 2023. Not because prescribers turned hostile. Because patients lapsed.

The switch happens at the pharmacy counter and on the EOB statement, not in the exam room. When out-of-pocket on a branded biologic runs 4–6× a biosimilar at copay reset, even a loyal prescriber and a willing patient can't hold the line if the patient doesn't know the bridge program exists, can't navigate the prior authorization, and isn't going to call the hub on their own. That is the structural risk in any specialty biologic transition, and it is what passive defense — relying on prescriber goodwill and rebate contracts alone — fails to address.

What actually held volume at AbbVie's Humira through biosimilar entry was a different posture. Direct-to-patient retention. Aggressive co-pay assistance. Hub services that handled the prior auths and the appeals. Bridge therapy when payer step-edits forced a biosimilar trial. AbbVie went into biosimilar entry having internalized the Remicade lesson, and 18 months in they still held roughly 70% of US Humira volume — far above the 30–40% pre-launch consensus. That is the playbook that traveled.

## The three-track defense

The recommendation here is not novel. It's the synthesis of two industry analogs (Humira and Stelara) plus an explicit reaction to the third (Remicade). Three tracks running in parallel, sequenced by what each defends and when it activates.

**Track 1 — Defend.** Patient retention. Make switching cost more than staying. The work here is unglamorous and decisive: raise the co-pay assistance cap, broaden eligibility, extend duration. Intensify hub services so prior authorizations turn around in days, not weeks, and so denied claims get appealed automatically. Build a bridge / free-drug program for patients caught in payer-mandated biosimilar step-throughs that fail. Layer in patient-direct retention communications — adherence support, peer connection, EOB explainers — that keep patients on therapy through the friction window. This is the Humira playbook applied to this brand.

**Track 2 — Shift.** Commercial weight to the adjacent indication. The instinct under pressure is to pull back from offense and over-fund defense. That is wrong here. The adjacent launch in 6 months is part of the defense, not separate from it. It diversifies the volume mix, gives the brand a story beyond the established indication, and creates a second franchise that can absorb pressure on the first. Stelara is the analog: J&J grew gastroenterology share of total Stelara volume from 30% to 55% by sequenced indication expansions, and that mix shift is what made biosimilar penetration in IBD forecast 20–30 points lower than in dermatology. The new launch must stay fully funded. Differentiated, not additive. Field redeployment designed before launch month, not after.

**Track 3 — Preserve.** Loyalty + access. Lock down the strategic perimeter while there's still time. Multi-year payer rebate contracts negotiated and signed before biosimilar entry — every month of a longer lock is incremental defense. KOL evidence platform: real-world evidence, registry data, post-hoc analyses, sub-population reads. Switch-back program in place for patients whose biosimilar trial fails. Sub-indication contracting so that the deepest defense lands where switching friction is highest (biologic-naive starts in dermatology behave differently than long-tenured patients in gastroenterology, and the spend mix should reflect that).

The three tracks run in parallel, but they activate at different times. Tracks 1 and 3 begin now. Track 2 weight-shift activates at month 6 with the adjacent launch. Stage gates at months 3, 6, and 12 tied to adjacent-launch traction and biosimilar uptake signals — so the spend mix can be tuned as real data comes in.

## The budget question

Senior leadership won't ratify the strategy on logic alone. The first question is going to be: *what does this cost, and where does it come from?*

The honest answer is that heavying patient services costs in the range of $32–70M in incremental annual spend across the four levers — co-pay program expansion ($15–35M), hub intensification ($8–15M), bridge / free-drug ($5–12M), and patient-direct retention comms ($4–8M). The ranges are wide because the actual figure depends on biosimilar timing, payer-mandate intensity, and how aggressive the program design is. But the order of magnitude is clear: this is a tens-of-millions commitment, not a hundreds-of-millions one.

Where it comes from matters more than how much it costs. The reallocation envelope is $38–78M and lives almost entirely in three places. HCP promotional spend on the established indication can absorb a $20–40M reduction without weakening the franchise — the loyal HCPs at 12+ months of Rx history rarely flip on rep frequency alone, and reach to lapsed or low-decile prescribers is the lowest-yield use of field time. DTC on the established indication can absorb another $15–30M — mature awareness is high, and the marginal DTC dollar buys little incremental acquisition this late in lifecycle. Non-critical medical affairs activity (small-conference presence, redundant advisory boards) can absorb the remaining $3–8M.

The key budget call is the one that *isn't* a cut: the adjacent indication launch spend stays fully funded. This is where the temptation to draw from offense is strongest, and where the Stelara analog says no most clearly. The launch *is* part of the defense.

The slide that survives the meeting is the one that says: *We're not adding budget. We're moving $35–55M from where it's no longer earning to where the brand is most exposed. The adjacent launch stays fully funded.*

## What the industry data points to, in priority order

If senior leadership has to pick where the budget goes first, the priority is unambiguous from the precedents:

1. **Co-pay assistance expansion + bridge program.** The single most defensible item to bring forward. Direct Humira analog with measured impact: ~70% of volume retained 18 months in, ~$4–5B defended vs. consensus base case. If you can fund only one thing, this is it.
2. **Hub services intensification.** Second priority. Lower spend, faster ROI, also defensible by Humira benchmark. Hub abandonment rates differ 10+ points between best-in-class and weak operations — that gap is where patients lapse.
3. **Adjacent indication launch — full funding, do not cut.** Stelara analog. The offense is part of the defense.
4. **Payer rebate / contracting team augmentation.** Even modest investment here pays back in lock duration. Multi-year contracts signed pre-biosimilar are what bought Humira its window.
5. **Reduce HCP-side and DTC spend on the established indication** to fund the above. Not a cut to the brand. A redeployment to where the brand is most exposed.

## Three things to be ready to defend

The conversation in the leadership review will land on three pressure points. Worth being ready.

The 6-month adjacent launch window is tight, and the 3-track defense compresses it further by layering Track 1 readiness work onto pre-launch field prep. If field readiness slips past week 16 of the launch ramp, Track 1 has to be funded from reserves rather than redeployment, and the budget story changes.

The sub-indication granularity matters more than aggregate numbers suggest. Switching friction in rheumatology, dermatology, and gastroenterology is materially different — biologic-naive starts in derm look nothing like long-tenured Crohn's patients in gastro. The patient-services and payer plays should be tilted to where friction is highest, and the validation read (by-specialty IQVIA NPA + the firm's own brand analytics) is the work to firm up before any commitment to specific spend mix by indication.

The payer-mandate risk is the wild card. If a major PBM goes biosimilar-exclusive on commercial book in the first 6 months post-entry, bridge program cost runs above range. The pre-modeled scenario is +$8–15M in additional bridge spend, offsetable by deeper HCP-side cuts. But it is the one variable most outside the brand's control.

## What this is — and isn't

This is a working POV. Not a finished plan. The numbers are directional ranges grounded in published precedents and industry benchmarks; the firm's brand finance team will sharpen them with internal spend mix data, IQVIA reads, and payer-segment specifics. The analogs are real. The reasoning is portable. The recommendation is committed enough to anchor the next conversation.

What this is *for* is the meeting tomorrow. The one where senior leadership asks how the brand is going to navigate the next 18 months and three things converging at once. Going in with nothing is worse than going in with this. Going in with this is what makes the next conversation possible.

Defend. Shift. Preserve. Three tracks, sequenced. $35–55M redeployment, fully self-funded. Adjacent launch protected. Hold 60–70% of patient volume through biosimilar entry — Humira benchmark.

That's the working POV.
